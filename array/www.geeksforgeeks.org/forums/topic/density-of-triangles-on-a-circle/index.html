    There are an “infinite points” on the circumference of a circle. Therefore, we cannot use combinatorics to calculate the probability of a right angled triangle among random 3 points on a circle. However, we know there an “infinite” pair of 2 points which are diametrically opposed on a circle. For each pair of “infinite” diametrically opposed points on circle, there should always exist a third point which adheres to a^^2 + a ^^ 2 = (d/2)^^2. So how can we say that the probability of a Right angle triangle on a circle is nearly zero.

    I talked to my professor of mathematics at Tufts University(I won’t mention her name , but I didn”t get permission) ,who is very famous, about the probability of a right angle triangle in a circle. Here is the quoted answer.

    “Given three points on a circle, A, B, C, the angle of the segments AB BC is half of the angle measured in the circle from A to C by geometric properties of the circle. This angle is 90 degrees precisely when A and C are the two ends of a diameter. So assume you choose A and B at random. In order to get a right angle, C can only be one point. The probability that you choose that point is 0. What this means is the following: assume that instead of having only one point as the answer, you could have a whole arc in the circle as the answer. Then you would get as probability the quotient of the length of that arc by the length of the whole circle.

    For example, if you want to find the probability that the angle is at most 45 degrees and A and B are more than a quarter of a circle apart, then you can choose C up to a quarter of a circle apart from A either clockwise or counterclockwise. So you can choose C in half of the circle. So the probability is ½.

    For the case of the angle exactly 90, you can only choose one point, that is an interval of length 0. You divide by the length of the circle and you get 0.”
